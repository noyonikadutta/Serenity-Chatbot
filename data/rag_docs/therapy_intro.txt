Therapy Introduction:
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a structured, time-limited, problem-focused form of psychotherapy. It helps people identify and challenge unhelpful thoughts and behaviors. CBT involves changing thinking patterns, like recognizing distortions and gaining a better understanding of others' behavior. It also involves changing behavioral patterns, such as facing fears and learning to calm one's mind.

Mindfulness:
Mindfulness is the practice of purposely bringing one's attention in the present moment without judgment. Key components include awareness and acceptance. It is often used to reduce stress and anxiety.

Emotional Regulation:
Emotional regulation refers to the ability to exert control over one's own emotional state. It involves minimizing the intensity of emotions, or changing the duration of the emotion. Strategies include reappraisal, suppression, and acceptance.

Active Listening:
Active listening requires the listener to fully concentrate, understand, respond, and then remember what is being said. It is a key component of effective therapy.

Validation:
Validation is the recognition and acceptance of another person's thoughts, feelings, sensations, and behaviors as understandable. It does not mean agreement, but rather acknowledgment.
